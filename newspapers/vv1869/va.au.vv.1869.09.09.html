To the Readers of the Virginian
(Column 01)
Summary: John H. Hewitt takes leave of Valley Virginian readers as he resigns his position on the editorial staff of the paper.
(Names in announcement: John H. Hewitt)
Prospects of the South
(Column 01)
Summary: Citing numerous economic advantages of the southern states, the author claims that the South is once again on the rise. If fact, according to his figures and other evidence, the South will soon not only rival, but surpass the New England states in manufacturing and agricultural pursuits. This prosperity, despite current oppression and the devastation of farms and cities, proves that the southerner will "not be shackled" and will once again rise to greatness.
Full Text of Article:

Day after day we are gathering evidence that the South will at no distant period become the creditor and the North the debtor. The day of her greatness is beginning to dawn and a brilliant future is awaiting her, not only in successful agricultural pursuits, but in the manufacture of staples. The Charleston News predicts that for 1869 the cotton States will show more true prosperity than any other section of the world. It estimates the present cotton crop at 3,000,000 bales.--The average value of all the crops will amount to sixty four dollars per head of the population. South Carolina, with all her drawbacks, will yield products this year to the amount of $50,000,000, or an average of $74.62 per head in a population of 670,000. The same journal remarks: "There are now no less than 200,000 whites cultivating cotton with their own hands;" probably referring to the uplands, as the seaboard and island cotton plantations are mostly worked by negroes. Years ago it was a rare thing to see a white laborer in the fields of South Carolina; it is different now, the white has become the competitor of the black, and a powerful one, too. The rice crop also bids fair for a rich yield, and sugar cane is flourishing.

Now to these rich annual products add the fact that cotton and woolen factories are springing up in all parts of the South, producing large amounts of fabrics from the material, as it were, at their very doors, who will deny that the manufacturing interests of the North will not suffer? It is the vision of this growing greatness of the Southern States that has given birth to the undying hatred, the offspring of jealousy of the people of the New England States. The State of Virginia, though desolated by the iron car of war--her garden spots made waste places, and her cities burnt, is again springing up phoenix like from her ashes, and developing her greatness. Her agricultural and mineral resources are exhaustless, and make her of commercial importance of the world. Oppression may do its worst--she will develop herself, for the genius and energy of her people cannot be shackled. The State of Georgia is already competing favorably with the New England States in her cotton and woolen fabrics, saying nothing of her iron works; in fact, the attention of capitalists is being turned toward the South with her numerous power working streams, rich soil and balmy climate; and it is not unreasonable to suppose that even the money of the speculative Yankees will ere long be invested in Southern factories and foundries.


Important
(Column 01)
Summary: The paper reports that Attorney General Hoar decided that the test-oath should not be given to members of the Virginia legislature.
Lo, the Poor Workingmen!
(Column 01)
Summary: Citing Horace Greeley's assessment of the prosperity of the working man, the author rejects any notion that workers are doing "well." Rather, he claims that increased wages make no difference when the cost of living has exceeded earning potential. Further, the author shows resentment for the rich "bond-holder," whose wealth only grows while the worker is denied "equal" rights and privileges.
Full Text of Article:

Horace Greeley is of the opinion that the working people are doing well when they receive two, three or four dollars per day. So they would be doing well if articles of consumption were at a reasonable price and the paper trash of the government equal to gold. A laboring man can now buy less for four dollars than he could for two in anti bellum days.--The poor fellow is starving even with good wages, and all Greeley's philosophy can't "make the pot boil." The worst of it is--his poverty is gaining on him every year, while the rich bond-holder doubles his golden income. Is this the government that holds up to the world equal rights and equal privileges?


The Test Oath. Attorney General Hoar's Opinion
(Column 03)
Summary: The paper prints Attorney-General Hoar's decision in the Virginia test-oath dispute. Hoar holds that since the election has been conducted, legislators-elect are the legal representatives of the people. It is too late to impose additional obligations upon them. Congress may decide not to admit the state under the current legislature, but military authorities cannot demand test-oaths.

County Fair
(Column 01)
Summary: The tournament to be held at the next county fair will be "an extra splendid display of ladies and knights in costume." A coronation and fancy ball will be conducted "according to the customs of the age of chivalry."
Our Schools
(Column 01)
Summary: Staunton's excellent schools will open soon for the fall term. Three are for young ladies: The Virginia Female Institute, Rev. R. Phillips president; The Augusta Female Seminary, Mary Baldwin principal; and the Wesleyan Female Institute, Rev. W. A. Harris president. "All these schools have strong corps of instructors and enjoy much popularity with the public. They hold out many inducements to parents and guardians, not only for the intellectual improvements of the pupils but their health and moral well-being. Staunton possesses attractions itself; it is beautifully situated, romantic scenery, pure air and refined society. There are churches of almost every denomination--all of them well attended."
(Names in announcement: Rev. R. Phillips, Mary Baldwin, Rev. W. A. Harris)
