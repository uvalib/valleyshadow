  • John Scott to Rev Samuel Hunt, December 1, 1865
    In this report to Samuel Hunt, Scott discusses a variety of local issues, including the pay rate for black mechanics in Staunton and the status of Sunday schools in the city. He describes the opposition of local whites to the educational mission of the AMA in the Valley as severe. A complete monthly report is attached to the letter.
  • John Scott to W. E. Whiting, December 9, 1865
    Scott offers a typical request for materials, but he stresses the need for candles and lamps as the classrooms suffer from inadequate lighting.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, December 18, 1865
    In a typical report to Samuel Hunt, Scott focuses on the recent acquisition of a "Rebel Camp stove" which has helped to provide heat in his classroom. He comments on the beauty of the "'Alleghany' Mts" and mentions a "young man" who has been buying "condemned property" in the Valley and assisting the AMA in Staunton.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, December 20, 1865
    Frustrated with the slow distribution of key resources to AMA schools in Staunton, Scott discusses the hardships of teaching in the Valley. He highlights his efforts to reform the "drinking" boys in the town and says that local whites have very little interest in "making anything of a 'nigger.'"
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, January 2, 1866
    Scott provides a summary report concerning the teachers in Staunton. He talks about their qualifications and what they have been able to accomplish in Staunton. He attacks C. E. Dewey for being too abrasive with local whites. He states that Dewey told one Southerner, "You are worms under our feet."
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, January 2, 1865
    Scott composes a letter to accompany a monthly report. The letter outlines school activities during the Christmas holidays and highlights the contributions local blacks made to help acquire additional buildings to be converted into schoolhouses. Scott also elaborates on the success of the AMA Sunday schools in Staunton.
  • John Scott to W. E. Whiting, January 2, 1866
    Letter forwarding bill and requesting immediate reimbursement for funds expended in Staunton.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, January 11, 1866
    Anxious to start an "Industrial School" for blacks in Staunton, Scott remarks that the growth in the number of students attending elementary schools precludes the establishment of specialized institutions in the city, as teachers cannot be spared. He adds that the community is in desperate need of material assistance from the AMA, commenting on the deplorable living conditions of many black families.
  • John Scott to W. E. Whiting, January 12, 1866
    Standard request for schoolbooks (largely arithmetic textbooks).
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, January 25, 1866
    John Scott provides a summary of many of his day-to-day chores and mentions a recent hunting trip, saying that the excursion was his first day of leisure in Staunton. The letter also serves to highlight his dissatisfaction with C. E. Dewey, an AMA teacher under his supervision.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, June 30, 1866
    Reflecting on eight months of teaching, Scott comments on the improvements in the general living conditions of blacks in Staunton. Mentioning an "Exhibition" put on by his students, he proclaims that the "Declamation" could "not have been excelled by any white school at the north."
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, February 3, 1866
    Scott provides a brief and informal report concerning the schools in Staunton. Highlighting the success of the AMA's educational mission in Staunton, Scott argues that the Association needs to acquire more classrooms.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, February 28, 1866
    Scott complains about the removal of William L. Coan from his AMA post in Virginia and challenges the assertion that the Valley's schools are not "promising," providing statistics about the schools in Staunton to support his argument.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, March 13, 1866
    Scott laments the removal of William L. Coan from his position with the AMA in Virginia, arguing that Coan was a necessary and exceedingly helpful agent of the Association in the area. Commenting on financial troubles, Scott argues that it might be possible for teachers to board with "colored families" as a means to cut down on costs.
  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, March 17, 1866
    John Scott begins his missive to Samuel Hunt by acknowledging that he is a bad speller. He apologizes for his imperfections, but he maintains that his frustrations with spelling make him even more eager to correct bad habits in his students' writing. Scott then transitions into a discussion of the condition of his school, arguing that the warm weather will certainly draw students out of the classroom and into the fields, making it necessary to reorganize classes. He adds that the teachers are tired and in need of a vacation, having worked diligently for many weeks.

  • John Scott to Samuel Hunt, April 2, 1866
    In this informal report, John Scott begins by saying that his students have been progressing quite remarkably. He then complains about a black "Woman Preacher" in Staunton who had persuaded many citizens to leave the "Colored Methodist Ch." He says that the situation has threatened the financial integrity of the AMA's operations in Staunton. Scott mentions that he traveled to Lexington, Virginia, and gave a speech on "Free Labor and Its Reward," concluding that the schools in that town were doing very well. He also discusses boarding arrangements for teachers in Staunton.

  • William L. Coan to Samuel Hunt, November 4, 1865
    Coan begins his letter with a discussion of his recent journeys to Woodstock, New Market, and Harrisonburg, arguing that a mission team should be sent to Harrisonburg, a community where he claims "Rebellion" is "deeply seated." He notes that there "are Lutheran Churches scattered all along this Valley," and argues that a representative from that denomination might be best fit to begin work in Harrisonburg. Excited about the prospect of new schools in Staunton and surrounding communities, Coan exclaims, "I have seen no place where Educational influences are more needed and demanded than in this Valley. We owe it to the Rebels that we throw in the Northern "Yankee" Christian Element here." Though hopeful that Northern aid might quash the social injustice associated with "Rebel" rule in the Valley, Coan criticizes the "Bureau" and "Mr Beecher" for "doing much to encourage these Rebels in their course" and for "doing much which tends to discourage the real Unionists at the South." In one passage of the letter, he refers to the Shenandoah Valley as the "Valley of Humiliation" and a "sink-hole of Iniquity."

Commenting on Southern whites' changing perception of the freedmen's schools in Staunton, Dewey argues that though the AMA teachers are "without military protection we have had no trouble + I think the feeling towards 'Nigger Teachers' the little which we have the honor to receive, grows less bitter every day."

    Responding to Whipple's criticism that he has been unwilling to find a more cost effective means of managing AMA facilities in Staunton, Tukey maintains that the "principle causes of your expense here over and above other places was because there was no confiscated building here in which the Teachers could get to live neither did the Colored people have any Church or other building in which we could hold your Schools."
  • F. S. Tukey to W. E. Whiting, April 7, 1866
    This is a standard bill for teachers' and "servants'" board.
  • F. S. Tukey to George Whipple, April 7, 1866
    In his communication to W. E. Whiting, Tukey lists recent expenses associated with the AMA's educational mission in Staunton.
  • F. S. Tukey to W. E. Whiting, May 11, 1866
    Tukey writes to Whiting asking what should be done with a collection of lamps recently received from AMA offices in the North.
  • F. S. Tukey to W. E. Whiting, July 1, 1866
    Tukey reflects on the AMA's efforts to establish schools for freedmen in Staunton and concludes that "firm and prudent management has overcome much . . . prejudice and forced even Rebs to respect us."
