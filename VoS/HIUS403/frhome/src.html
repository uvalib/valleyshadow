Personal Narratives

  • Gen. Couch's Official Report

    This was Gen. Couch's report of the events early skirmished and preparations for the invasion. He also includes a summary of his failed attempts to contact Averell.

  • Official Records

    Official Records concerning the burning of Chambersburg and the events surrounding it. Primarily reports from Union generals.

  • The Great Invasion by Jacob Hoke

    Jacob Hoke was a Chambersburg merchant and in this definitive account of Lee's Invasion, he includes an appendix analyzing the reasons and justifications for the burning.

  • The Burning of Chambersburg by Rev. Schenck

    Selections from a widely circulated book written shortly after the burning by Chambersburg minister.

  • Gen. McCausland's Account

    A account given by Gen. McCausland several years after the incident.

  • Gen. Early's Reasons

    This was a published letter in Jacob Hoke's book from Gen. Early. The letter states his reasons for selecting Chambersburg.

  • A Confederate Soldier's Account

    Another confederate perspective of the burning from the common soldier.

  • Mr. Slinguff's Letters

    Fielder Slingluff was a Confederate in the First Maryland Cavalry at the burning.

  • Why Early Burned Chambersburg

    B. L. Wynn was a member of the signal corps under Gen. Early that sent the communications between Early and Lee. In his account he reproduces several of the messages he sent for Early concerning the Chambersburg.

  • Letter from Charles Smith to Lieut. McDermott

    A letter from Asst. Sgn. Charles Smith requesting twenty days leave from duty to help his family find a new home in Chambersburg after theburning.

  • Diary of Rachel Cormany

    Rachel Cormany was a Canadian born woman brought to Chambersburg in 1862 by her husband Samuel. Soon after their arrival, Samuel enlisted in the 16th Pensylvainia Volunteer Cavalry, leaving Rachel alone in Chabersburg, lodging in the house of Jonathan Plough on Front Street. Rachel's accounts speak to the difficulties a women of the day would face as well as her spiritual life.

    • December 26, 1862

      Rachel speaks of receiving financial help from both her Aunt Elenor and from her husband Samuel in the form of his county bounty.

    • February 1, 1863

      Rachel complains of increasing interest, suggesting that her bills are backing up with no consistent source of income.

    • February 18, 1863

      Rachel wishes for ever increasing faith in her time of hardship away from her husband Samuel.

    • June 17, 1863

      Rachel describes an incident in which a young female thief visits her house in the gues of a preacher's daughter.

    • August 6, 1864

      Description of the burning and the mood of the town afterwards.

  • Diary of William Heyser

    Heyser, a Chambersburg businessman, kept a diary that detailed the events of both invasions of 1862 and 1863.

Newspapers

  • "Welcome Surprise.", Franklin Repository and Transcript, January 12, 1859, p. 5, c. 3

    Members of the Lutheran congregation improved their minister's home and property in his absence, to the value of about $200.

  • "Chambersburg Female Seminary.", Valley Spirit, February 2, 1859, p. 4, c.6

    Editorial praises school, urges public support

  • "Ladies Attention.", Valley Spirit, June 8, 1859, p. 5, c. 2

    Editorial suggests a "pleasant duty" for the ladies of the county: decorate the yard in the rear of the courthouse with flowers and shrubbery.

  • "The Flower Queen", Valley Spirit, June 15, 1859, p. 5, c. 2

    Review of performance at Franklin Hall

  • "Farmers and Mechanics.", Valley Spirit, June 15, 1859, p. 5, c. 2
    Election of officers.

  • "Franklin County Agricultural Society.", Valley Spirit, June 22, 1859, p. 5, c. 2

    Organization revived and fully organized; plans laid for county fair in October.

  • "Franklin County.", Valley Spirit, July 13, 1859, p. 5, c. 9

    Comparison of current agricultural production to 1850 census figures. "These facts go to show what an excellent county, for agricultural purposes, Franklin is, and there is no one at all well acquainted with its quality of good lands that does not believe but that by improvement in cultivation, the agricultural products can be increased 50 per cent."

  • "Very Pleasant.", Franklin Repository and Telegraph, July 20, 1859, p. 5, c. 3

    Pronounces the July 14th concert of the Chambersburg Mechanics' Brass Band satisfactory

  • "Paris Fashions For August.", Valley Spirit, August 17, 1859, p. 8 c. 1

    A description from Galignani's Messenger of the latest in fashions from Paris. Explains what should and should not be worn together, as well as what clothing is most popular.

  • "Dramatic Entertainmnet.", Valley Spirit, Decmeber 21, 1859, p. 5, c. 2

    Some "public spirited" young men will offer dramatic entertainmnet in the public hall for the benefit of the public library. The Chambersburg Band and a "fine orchestra for the occasion" will perform.

  • "The Franklin Railroad.", Valley Spirit, February 8, 1860, p. 5, c. 2

    Line between Chambersburg and Greencastle is complete; "a train of Cars, for carrying passengers and freight, commenced running, regularly on Monday last." Complaints that railroad is charging passengers more than law allows; taxpayer subsidy noted.

  • "Sermon for the Benefit of the Poor.", Franklin Repository and Transcript, February 15, 1860, p. 4, c. 5:

    Report on sermon of Rev. Samuel Philips on John Wesley and the importance of giving to the poor.

  • "Rosedale Seminary.", Franklin Repository and Transcript, July 4, 1860, p. 1, c. 4

    Commends the teachers and students of this female seminary.

  • "As a General Rule...", Franklin Repository and Transcript, July 11, 1860, p. 3, c. 2

    Expresses the opinion that girls should spend time on modern languages, instead of ancient ones, and that girls only need to know arithmetic, algebra, and some geometry.

  • "A Sensible Young Lady", Franklin Repository and Transcript, July 18, 1860, p. 3, c. 3

    Describes how a lazy young girl became healthier and more attractive after she started getting exercise by doing housework.

  • "The Crops.", Franklin Repository and Transcript, July 18, 1860, p. 5, c. 2

    Points out that only six counties in the entire country produced more wheat than Franklin--most were in New York except for Lancaster, Pennsylvania. Franklin county produced 837,062 bushels. Also provides a summary of the corn, potato, oat, and hay crops, all of which are bountiful.

  • "'Infanticide.'", Valley Spirit, July 3, 1861, p. 2, c. 2

    Mary Starlipper, an employee of Fisher's Hotel, gave birth to a child and concealed it in a bucket. She claimed it was still-born, but the medical examiner found that the child was born alive. She will be arrested and taken to prison.

  • "Soldier's Wives.", Franklin Repository

    A local woman praises the strength of soldier's wives in their struggles during the war.

  • "Camp Meeting.", Valley Spirit, August 7, 1861, p. 3, c. 1:

    Announces that a "Union Camp Meeting" of the Churches of God will be held on August 15.

  • "Horrible.", Valley Spirit, September 4, 1861, p. 5, c. 1

    A woman named Caroline Toms is to be arrested for fornication and bastardy and the concealing the birth of a child by leaving it in a privy. The child was determined to have been still born.

  • "Where Are We Drifting?", Valley Spirit, November 27, 1861, p. 2, c. 4

    Bemoans recent crimes in Franklin County, including the theft of some hams by a "contraband" and a rash of shoplifting by women.

  • "True Patriotism." Valley Spirit, December 18, 1861, p. 5, c. 2

    Asserts that, despite what the Chambersburg Times may write, the people of Franklin are patriotically supporting the Union cause as evidenced by the ladies of the German Reformed Church and their charity activities.

  • "Sunday School Festival." Valley Spirit, January 1, 1862, p. 5, c. 1

    Describes a show put on by the Sunday School in connection with the Lutheran Church to raise money for the "Orphans Home".

  • "Presentation." Valley Spirit, January 8, 1862, p. 5, c. 1

    Members of the congragations under a pastor presented said pastor with a new horse on New Years Day.

  • "Glad to Hear It", Valley Spirit, January 22, 1862, p. 5, c. 2

    The Ladies of the Volunteer Aid Society of Mercersburg gave a box of clothing to company F., located at "Camp Wood", Kentucky.

  • "What We Buy And What We Sell.", Valley Spirit, January 29, 1862, p. 4, c. 2:

    Lists the goods imported to and exported from Franklin County on the Cumberland Valley Railroad and argues that these figures prove that "Chambersburg is not a 'one-horse town.'"

  • "Our Patriotic Ladies.", Valley Spirit, July 30, 1862, p. 5, c. 1

    The Ladies Aid Society, working out of the Associate Reformed Church, has been occupied with the "noble, patriotic and christian" task of providing clothing and other goods for sick soldiers. The editors praise them highly, and argue that the ladies of Chambersburg will compare favorably to those of any other community.

  • Valley Spirit, October 15, 1862, "The Rebel Raid" p. 5, c. 1.

    Short article recounts the invasion of "a large force of Rebel Cavalry", and what they took.

  • Valley Spirit, November 5, 1862, "The Invasion of Pennsylvania" p. 1, c. 3.

    More detailed article about the 1962 Invasion, including reprints of correspondance between Confederate Generals Robert E. Lee and J.E.B. Stuart.

  • "The Crops.", Franklin Repository and Transcript, July 22, 1863, p. 5, c. 2

    Due to rebel thievery and occupation, the Franklin County crop was much reduced in the harvest of 1863. Although the wheat harvest came out average, the harvest of clover hay, corn, and potatoes were all greatly hinderd, due to incufficient ploughing, as well as loss of stock and horses.

  • "Rebel Invasion," Valley Spirit, July 6, 1864

  • "Rebel Invasion," Valley Spirit, July 14, 1864

  • "Rebel Invasion," Valley Spirit, July 20, 1864

  • "The Respository Re-Appears", The Franklin Repository, August 24, 1864.

    The editors of the Franklin Repository explain the losses incurred by the newspaper but insist to their readers not allow the burning to deter them from winning the war.

  • "Burning of Chambersburg", The Franklin Repository, August 24, 1864.

    The author reprimands the South for mercilessly destroying Chambersburg when northern armies have consistently passed peacefully through the southern homefront.

  • "Local Items", The Franklin Repository, August 24, 1864.

    Provides an optimistic view of the future of Chambersburg since the burning. Calls upon the burned town's citizens to rebuild its businesses before the "adventurers and sharpers take their places".

  • "The Future", The Valley Spirit, August 31, 1864.

    Describes the bleak and desolate current state of Chambersburg and warns its citizens of colder seasons ahead.

  • "The New York Papers", The Franklin Repository, August 31, 1864.

    Expresses bitterness toward the editors of the New York Tribune and New York Herald for the "heartlessness exhibited" in their views on the burning of Chambersburg.

  • The Valley Spirit, August 31, 1864

    The paper's office was burned in the raid. It took the Valley Spirit more than a month to return to production. The August 31, 1864 edition was the first edition to discuss the burning and its affect.

  • "How They Talk", Valley Spirit, October 12, 1864.

    Argues that Governor Curtain has not made good on promises to end the war and blames him for the burning of Chambersburg.

  • "Union Men!", The Franklin Repository, November 2, 1864.

    Calls upon the voters in support of a unified nation to vote for Abraham Lincoln because a vote for General McClellan could come "at the cost of dismemberment".

  • "Democrats to the Rescue", Valley Spirit, November 2, 1864.

    Challenges the Democratic faithful to garner votes for McClellan and not to rest until the end of the election.

  • "The Late Election", Valley Spirit, November 16, 1864.

    Expresses bitterness toward Lincoln's victory in the election of 1886 but insists that Democratic supporters continue their efforts to end the war.
