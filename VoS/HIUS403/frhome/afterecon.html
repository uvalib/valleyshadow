The election of 1864 was a major issue of discussion during the months after the burning and offered the citizens of Chambersburg an opportunity to begin refocusing on the national picture after having beed occupied with the burning of their home town for so long. The presidential race between Abraham Lincoln and General George McClellan was an especially heated topic of debate. Some Chambersburg residents complained that the war had continued for too long and that it was becoming too violent. General McClellan, who advocated ending the war as soon as possible, was a favorite among such individuals. To them, a victory by General McClellan meant a quick means of achieving peace. A reelection of President Lincoln would lead to more unnecessary battles and violence. The Valley Spirit, a local Democratic newspaper, contended that "four years more of such rule will completely bankrupt and destroy the Country".

On the other hand, most of the citizens of Chambersburg were insistent on a northern victory, regardless of how long the war or how vicious the battles. They felt that their town would have been lost for nothing if the North conceded victory to the South. Alexander K. McClure, a prominent politician, an acquaintance of Abraham Lincoln, and the owner of The Franklin Repository, insisted that the war must continue at all costs until the North had one and the nation was reunited. The Repository, in an article printed the week before the election on November 2, 1864, states that "if the national verdict is for Lincoln, as it surely must be, it is a declaration that the war, now to all human appearances on the threshold of final and complete success, shall be prosecuted until the despairing traitors yield to the majesty of the laws and the unity of the States."

The fact that a large majority of the Chambersburg population supported Lincoln and a northern victory at the cost of a longer war demonstrates that the Chambersburg citizens were able to maintain a perspective on the war. It further illustrates the the citizens had begun to move forward with their lives since the burning. The economy was beginning to revive itself and Chambersburg was quickly recovering from its losses. Though the ashes of burned homes and buildings were constant reminders of the burning, the citizens of Chambersburg were unyielding in their attempts to rebuild their town and defeat the South.
