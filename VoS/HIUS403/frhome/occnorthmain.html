By June of 1864, Chambersburg had already lived through two occupations by Confederate troops, which distinguished it from most other Northern towns. Each invasion onto the home front, culminating in the burning of the town in 1864, was more invasive, more intrusive,and brought the battlefront closer to the homes of Chambersburg residents. The way that citizens viewed the invading armies, as well as the actual damage inflicted by the raiders, reflected this progression towards a more total war. These early invasions were not total war, but they did bring enemy soldiers into the town square, into the homes of Chambersburg townspeople, and into their businesses.
