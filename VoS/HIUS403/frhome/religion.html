The residents of Franklin County led lives that were intrinsically bound to their religion. Regardless of the denomination of Christianity to which a person belonged, religion served as a tool through which morality was taught, as a place to find solac e, and most often as a major source of social interaction.

The people of the country could, every week, find short stories or just sayings in either the Valley Spirit or The Franklin Repository with clearly religious themes. Such articles spoke out on the proper way to teach virtues to the young, or the dangers of typical vices. No matter the exact message, multiple religious sayings throughout the papers of the day were common.

In a time when every week brought new reports on the increasing death toll of the war or on some new injured or captured soldier, religion served as a base by which Franklin County residents could ground themselves and protect themselves from the pains of life. Rachel Cormany, a Chambersburg resident, speaks to this look to religion in her diary. Again, religious support was often accomplished extensively through the newspapers. Such teachings could also be found in the sermons of the local preachers, many of whom were thanked heartily yearly for their efforts.

With little providing either a place around which to rally or even everyday social interaction, the churches of Franklin County served to fill these roles. Social events, such as pageants and sings were often organized by a church. At the same time, clothes and food drives for the soldiers and the poor were organized almost exclusively by the religious women of the county.

    Report on sermon of Rev. Samuel Philips on John Wesley and the importance of giving to the poor.

  • "Camp Meeting.", Valley Spirit, August 7, 1861, p. 3, c. 1:

    Announces that a "Union Camp Meeting" of the Churches of God will be held on August 15.

  • "True Patriotism." Valley Spirit, December 18, 1861, p. 5, c. 2

    Asserts that, despite what the Chambersburg Times may write, the people of Franklin are patriotically supporting the Union cause as evidenced by the ladies of the German Reformed Church and their charity activities.

  • "Sunday School Festival." Valley Spirit, January 1, 1862, p. 5, c. 1

    Describes a show put on by the Sunday School in connection with the Lutheran Church to raise money for the "Orphans Home".

  • "Presentation." Valley Spirit, January 8, 1862, p. 5, c. 1

    Members of the congregations under a pastor presented said pastor with a new horse on New Years Day.

  • "Our Patriotic Ladies.", Valley Spirit, July 30, 1862, p. 5, c. 1:

    The Ladies Aid Society, working out of the Associate Reformed Church, has been occupied with the "noble, patriotic and christian" task of providing clothing and other goods for sick soldiers. The editors praise them highly, and argue that the ladies of Chambersburg will compare favorably to those of any other community.
