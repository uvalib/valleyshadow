During the spring of 1866, nearly six hundred Chambersburg residents filed claims at the Chambersburg courthouse to report destroyed property that was burned during General McCausland's invasion on July 30, 1864. The claims report detailed descriptions of lost personal property and real estate. Two appraisers were appointed to assess the validity of the claims and award appropriate monetary amounts to individuals who lost property. On February 15, 1866, $500,000 was allocated to the residents of Chambersburg in relief of the burning by the General Assembly of Pennsylvania. The money was distributed pro rata at about thirty percent of the awarded amount.

The devastation of Chambersburg's burning on July 30, 1864, included losses of both personal property and real estate. Personal items including tools, food, furniture, and clothing as well as real estate ranging from houses to churches left many individuals without basic necessities and shelter. The total damage is estimated at three million dollars, of which $1,783,950 was real estate property. Although the upper classes lost more total property than the underprivileged, the poor undoubtedly suffered much more as a result of the burning. The poor residents did not have the money nor the resources to purchase new supplies or find new places to live; many were left homeless and struggled to regain economic stability. The richer families were able to survive by residing in hotels or living temporarily with friends in neighboring areas.

The claims also prove that the distinction between the battlefield and the homefront became less and less apparent as the war progressed. Throughout the beginning of the war, Chambersburg was able to distance itself from battles and occupation. However, as warfare became more militant and soldiers became less tolerant and more impatient, the northern homefront that once seemed safe suddenly was being inhabited by rebels. The occupations of 1862 and 1863 are examples of this trend toward destroying the supposed barrier between the warfront and the homefront. The claims provide explicit evidence that the residents of Chambersburg were not expecting such an invasion. Very few buildings survived and thousands of dollars in personal property were destroyed. Very few individuals responded to the threat of General McCausland; consequently, hardly any steps were taken to prevent the damage caused by the rebels.
