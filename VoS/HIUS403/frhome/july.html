The Burning of Chambersburg

he summer of 1864 changed Chambersburg forever. The residents had adapted to life on the border between the Union and the Confederacy. The small agricultural community had experienced its fair share of the war, as had many other northern border towns in Pennsylvania and Maryland. While many of its men were off in battle, the rest of the town tried to continue as normally as it could. Farmers went on working in the fields and merchants continued to keep their stores, all the while keeping on top of the war through personal letters and local newspapers.

But the war was not completely out of sight. Chambersburg was the military headquarters for the Department of the Susquehanna, under Brig. Gen. Darius Couch. Just outside of town were stationed thousands of Union soldiers. The railroad station was constantly filled with trains transporting ammunition, food, supplies, and equipment for army units. The memories of the rebel occupations were still fresh in many people's minds. They could not forget all of the cattle, food, dry goods, and clothes taken by the Confederates.

For three years this co-existence of a military sphere and a public sphere set the backdrop of daily life in Chambersburg. In July 1864, this would quickly change as the residents learned first hand the terrors of war when they became the targets of the Confederacy. The burning of Chambersburg finally initiated the North into the realities of war without any limitations.
