Employment Disputes

Employment disputes arose from a variety of reasons, and frequently the tenor of letters to the Bureau indicated that employers felt some resentment about having to adjust to a free labor force. Whites also often expressed the opinion that the Bureau was more worried about distributing aid to the freedmen than about helping them become gainfully employed, as the poster shown above demonstrates.

  • An indenture agreement created in 1850 between John H. Rippetoe and Punch Fox led to Bureau investigation in 1867.

  • An unspecified dispute led William Saulsbury to seek Bureau aid in 1867.

  • Annie E. Branham wrote the Bureau protesting wages that a Joshua Harris claimed she owed to him.

  • John N. Opie wrote to the Bureau to state that Robert Napper and Richard Vaughn had broken a labor contract with him.

  • Joshua Harris filed a claim against Mr. Jaccheri for unpaid wages.
