Valley Virginian


What's the Times!
vol. 1, no. 9, Janurary 31, 1866, pg. 2, col. 2
This question is often asked now by our people and it is a serous one, so serious to us that it is painful to attempt to answer to it. If President Johnson's policy had been carried out by Congress, our Country, the South, would have now been on the high road to prosperity and some hope of repairing the devastation of war could be entertained. It has not been carried out, but, has been opposed by the Radicals with consumate ability and spite.

When the armies of the Confederacy surrendered and our people accepted thepledges of Andres Johnson and Gen. Grant, they expected to be restored to all the rights and privileges of citizens of the United States, to which they renewed their allegiance. -- They accepted, in good faith, the results of hte war; gave up slavery and were prepared to work out the problem of free negro lavor, as best they could....

The Jacobin Radicals, in Congress, are doing their utmot to make a war of racss ; their speeches and their voes show it.-- Negro suffrage is to be enforced ad the negro placed ont only on an equality, but made superior to the white man. When the contest opens, as we hope it never may, the "times" in the South will indicate the extermination of the negro race.

All this and the confiscation laws make the times look very black, and we can see but one hopeful sign and that is in the old saying, that "the man who killed a dog for spite never saw him buried...."
