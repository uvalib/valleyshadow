Abuse

One of the many functions the Freedmen's Bureau addressed was the issue of justice. Following the end of the war, Southern whites resented both their northern occupiers and newly freed blacks. This resentment can be seen in newspapers, diaries, and letters, and one way whites released their aggression was by attacking freedmen. Victims rarely found protection or reparatiom from the civil athorities, which were often dominated by the same whites who had been the aggavators . With civil utlets closed, the local Freedmen's Bueau office appeared to be a haven for victims. The Bureau received letters from both blacks and whites complaining of attacks, mistreatment and other criminal actions. In dealing with these claims, however, the Bureau's hands were tied and the cases seldomly were addressed by the civil athorities. As a result assults, abuse, an thefts remained a common occurance in reconstruction Virginia.

  • Assault: Although the Civil War ended at Appomattox, violence still plauged the occupied South, and Augusta County was no exception. The Freedmen's Bureau in Staunton received numerous complaints and requests for help concerning assaults agianst both black and white residents.

  • Abuse: In the case of abuse, Thomas Jackson of the Bureau did try to intervene in a case of aledged child abuse. Abuse however, was not always directed toward childern. Violence toward women was also common.

  • Theft: With local law enforcement unexcessable to many, victims of thefts often turned to the Bureau for assistance and even formed local resistances groups when all else failed.
