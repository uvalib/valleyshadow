Contracts

Contracts arrived in the hands of the Bureau for a variety of reasons: advice, safe keeping, dispute, and so forth. Contracts had obviously been a rarity before the war, and were now a necessity as the South adjusted to a free labor force. As a result, contracts ranged from the simple to the complex, the fair to the unreasonable, and were entered into for every imaginable type of labor.

  • June 1, 1866: Sarah A. Craig wrote to the Bureau to protest claims made against her by a former slave, Ellen Taylor.

  • March 26, 1867: A sharecropping agreement between Abraham Watkins (c) and William J. McKee.

  • April 20, 1867: J. H. Hoffman hired the services of Richard Banheart.

  • May 10, 1867: G. W. McCutchen disputes Henry Alexander's claim to the estate of John P. M. McCutchen.

  • July 17, 1867: James Curtis, a black man, asks the Bureau to aid him in a dispute with John Dinkle.

  • August 4, 1867: Richard Banks wrote to the Bureau seeking aid in recovering wages being withheld from him by a former employer.

  • December 7, 1867: Statement of account between Dorcas Wait and a Mr. Burdett.

  • December 30, 1867: Copy forwarded to Bureau of a sharecropping contract between D. Kunkle and Reuben Gouldin (c).

  • March 24, 1868: Copy forwarded to the Bureau of a compromise over disputed land between John Bumgardner (col'd) and Echols, Bell, Catlett & Co.

  • May 8, 1868: William Meredith seeks wages being withheld by the Virginia Central Rail Road Company.

  • October 5, 1868: The Western Lunatic Asylum forwarded a copy of Nelson Walker's contract to the Bureau, apparently in response to a dispute over his dismissal from its employ.
