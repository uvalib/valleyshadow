Bureaucracy

The Bureau was established in March 3, 1865, and was directed by General O.O. Howard, who was appointed by President Andrew Johnson. It was staffed by approximately 900 agents throughout the South. Each of the ten states being reconstructed had an assistant commissioner. The assistant commissioner for Virginia was General Orlando Brown. Below the assistant commissioner were sub-assistant commissioners. Captain John A. McDonnell was the sub-assistant commissioner in charge of Captain Thomas P. Jackson's district, and was stationed in Winchester, VA, about 100 miles north of Staunton in the Shenandoah Valley.

The commissioner received a salary of $3,000 and was given bonds in the sum of $50,000. The assistant commissioner's salary was $2,500 and he received a bond of $20,000.

The Bureau was understaffed nationally, and this nature was reflected in Staunton as well. Thomas P. Jackson was the only agent in Augusta County for most of the Bureau's life. His responsibilities included caring for the welfare of the freedmen who numbered more than 5,000 in 1867, and some destitute whites. The accomplishments of its agents were further hampered by its highly bureaucratic nature.

As Reconstruction came to an end in Virginia, so did the Bureau. The Bureau in Staunton was ordered to close on December 21, 1868.
