March 29, 1862:

Received notice this morning that we would go to Fort Delaware at 10 o'clock but the hours passed without any sign of movement, of which we were not sorry, for we preferred to remain in the city of our friends, rather than go to a fort, shut off from all the world by water as well as by its walls and be among our enemies.

Such sympathy was expressed and so much excitement stirred up through the city, that the authorities feared another outbreak of the populace (as on the 19th of April 1861) if they moved the prisoners through the streets in daylight. The keepers of the prison expressed this opinion to our officers.

The morning was cloudy and soon commenced snowing. In the afternoon we were again ordered to be ready to leave and started between 4 and 5 o'clock, under the escort of the 111th Pa. regiment.

Stormy and cold as it was, a large crowd was assembled at the gate to witness our departure. As soon as the prisoners appeared, the crowd commenced waving handkerchiefs, shouting and hurrahing for Jeff Davis and the South. As we moved off, the crowd became perfectly uproarious, shouting, and hallooing at the top of their voices. Many ladies were among the throng, some standing in the storm, others in carriages, from which they waved us a silent adieu, sometimes with a fervent "God bless you."

We marched up Calvert street, thence along the latter to the boat landing in which we were to take passage for the Fort.

The police (appointees of General Dix) were in attendance; some of whom were very nice men, but the most of them bear a cruel and malignant hate towards the Rebels and delighted to taunt and vex them in every way.

Some ladies were standing in the hall one day, asked the prisoners for a button; one was thrown out and fell at their feet, a policeman standing by grabbed it up and refused to let her have it.

We were soon aboard, all crowded into the lower part of the boat where nothing could be seen, and steamed down the bay to Fort Delaware.
