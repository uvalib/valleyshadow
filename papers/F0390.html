Franklin County: Christian Geisel to Louisa Geisel, March 10, 1862

Summary:
Geisel writes to his family about being removed from the provost guard along the Rappahannock.

Louisa Geisel

March 10th 1862

Camp near Bell Blain Vir.

Dear Sister

I received your welcome letter of the 1rst alt. and was very happy to hear that you were all in good health.

We were relieved from provost guard on the [illeg.] and had a great parting time. All the people on the place white and black assembled together to take farewell from us. Mrs. Seddan gave us a boiled Ham and filed our Haversacks with [unclear: buishels ] to take along. they all said they were sorry that we were taken away and they wished us to come down to see them if we could. the parting scene was concluded by an old darky whom we called Uncle Tom asking blessings for us. i was send to the river a few days after we were relieved on buisness by Gen. Patrick. they all said they wished we were back again, while we were there. there was always some fun going on, but

[page 2]
[deleted: ] the Infantry thats there now were like a set of Quakers. they used to call us freemasons. We got to our camp near Whiteoak Church about an hour before sunset and again we got our shelters put up. a snowstorm commenced, which lasted all day sunday Feb [illeg.] we missed our good quarters very much in such rough weather but [illeg.] without doing us harm. We were to move camp on Feb. 22nd but were prevented by the bad weather until the 1rst of march on which day we moved. Our new camp is about one mile from Bell Blain landing within sight of the Potomac river; it is situated in a pine forest on the southside of a hill, and it is the most convienient campground we have had since we have been in Vir. We were busy all last week building log [illeg.] and cleaning up the ground, the whole camp looked like a carpentershop , and we are now all very comfortable fixed. I wish you could see

[page 3]
[illeg.] headquarters, they went home about [illeg.] clock, their road led them past our headquarters and they halted and played us a tune. they had a fiddle, tamperene, bones, and dry oncle. I expect we will go on picket again next thursday morning. I have not much other news to tell you. eveything is quiet [illeg.] think our army will move from here before spring for it is impossible to move now now for the roads down here are in very bad condition. it is raining every few days. it commenced this morning again. Co. L. + M of our Reg. are still at frederick, Md. yet but I believe our Col. has gone to Washington to get them down here. I am promised to get a Furlough but I dondt know how soon it will come; one of our men who is home on a Furlough now is expected back tomorrow. I would be very happy[illeg.]
