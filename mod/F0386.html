Franklin County: Christian Geisel to Louisa Geisel, August 5, 1862

Summary:
Geisel writes to his family to describe a series of artillery exchanges between Union and Confederate troops along the James River.

Louisa Geisel

August 5th 1862

Camp near Harrison Landing

Dear Sister!

Your welcome of July 30th reached me in good health, and I was glad to hear that you all still keep in good health. I see by your letter that you have a good deal of fun nowadays, but you never tell me any thing about boys being there. I suppose you girls must have all the fun to yourselves. We had a little fun here too last thursday night, playing balls with the rebels across the James River, but which is rather a dangerous play. About one o'clock on thursday night, the rebels opened fire on our camp with three batteries from the opposite side of the River which wakened us up from our sleep. the balls and shells whistled pretty sharp over our camp for a while. the shells just looked like skyrockets going through the air. We had been expecting it for several days, and the first Connecticut

[page 1]
artillery had planted their seigeguns along the banks of the river which soon silenced the rebel batteries when they opened on them. The river was lying full with vessels and schooners, but only one mast was hit by a ball. One man of our regiment of Company [illeg.] was killed by a shell. it only grazed his neck and shoulder and left a blue mark on it, but he was killed instantly. we found the shell in the morning behind our camp. it had not exploded. the man was sent home to be buried. Several other men and horses were killed in the different camps along the river. In the morning several regiments of our infantry were shipped across the river, but no rebels were to be found any more. they burned several houses along the river, behind which the rebels had their batteries stationed, a number of our troops cross the river every day since they have cut down all the woods along the river, so that the rebels cannot get near it any more without being seen. Early this morning we got orders to get ready for a march, and to

[page 2]
take two days forage and provisions along, but the order was countermanded again, and I don't think we will leave here today. There was heavy firing up the river this morning, and I have just now heard that General Sumner had a fight with the rebels this morning. he drove them over towards the the river, and then our gunboats opened on them, and they cut the rebels all to pieces. they ran and left everything behind them. I do not know how long we stay here yet, but I expect we will make a forward move before long. The weather is still very warm here, we had a pretty good shower last sunday, and I told my messmate, William J. Moore that if it rained as hard up in your neighbourhood it would spoil your calculations, as you said you expected to have a good deal of fun if it did not rain. Sister Hannah gave me fits in her last letter for plaking her about getting married. she said she guessed it was some of your stuff. I sent last week, which I suppose she has received before this

[page 3]
time. i have not seen any of the 104th lately, but I intend to pay them a visit some of these days if we don't move from here. they have not been at our camp yet. they say it is most too far to walk [illeg.] this warm weather. I have not seen Jonathan Harly lately either. they have moved their camp farther down the river. I will close hoping that this letter may find you all in as good health as I enjoy at present. give my regards to all our family. my compliments to your companions, and my respects to all inquiring friends. I hope to have the pleasure soon to hear from you again and remain

your affectionate Brother

Christian Geisel

Company H 6th Pennsylvania Cavalry Colonel R.H. Rush's Lancers Camp near Harrison's Landing of R. Virginia N.B. I should be very much pleased if you would do me the honor to send me your Photograph if it would not give you too much trouble.
