Franklin County: Christian Geisel to Louisa Geisel, May 12, 1863

Summary:
Geisel tells his sister about his regiment's encounters with Confederate troops during the Peninsula campaign.

Louisa Geisel

May 12th

Barnesville

Dear Sister!

Having some leisure moments this evening i will write you a few lines, which i hope may find you in as good health as i enjoy at present. I wrote you a letter while we were encamped near Hampton, but i have not heard from you since. We left Hampton on sunday the 4th instant and marched to Yorktown at sunset. we encamped there that night, it began to rain about midnight and our beds got pretty well soaked again [illeg.] morning as we had no tents along. on monday morning i went through

[page 1]
the forts and camps around Yorktown which the rebels evacuated on saturday night. they left most of their camp equippage and a great many cannons. as i came back towards our camp two men had just been blown up by shells which the [added: rebels] have buried all around their works in the ground and which explode if any body treads on them.

i had walked through the gate three times where one man was blown up but fortunately did not happen to tread on any of them. on Tuesday night we were in line of battle all night, as the rebels had outflanked our left wing. they were coming towards Yorktown again, but when they found that we were ready for them they retreated again. on saturday morning we marched from Yorktown

[page 2]
and arrived at Williamsburg in the evening, where a battle had been fought on monday. the town is full of wounded soldiers and rebel prisoners. the most of the women stayed in the town but there are not many men to be seen. we encamped about a mile beyond Williamsburg for the night and started off again on sunday morning at 4 O'clock. we marched as far as Barnesville about two miles from west point where we are now encamped, but i expect we will go on further before many days; one company of the 6th regular cavalry, who are brigaded with us, had a scrimmage yesterday. they routed four companies of rebel cavalry killing 18 and wounding 35 which they took prisoners, beside them that got away; today two of our men were found in the woods

[page 3]
tied to a tree and brains blown out. i do not know what regiment they belong to. I am in hopes that the war will soon be over, for i think the rebels will give up after we get Richmond. the weather has been good this while past, but it is getting very warm down here. i must now close my letter with my best respects to you all and all inquiring friends. i should like to hear from you soon.

Your affectionate Brother

Christian Geisel

direct your letter to Washington D.C.
