Augusta County: Diary of Andrew Brooks (1862)

1862

March, 1862

March 23, 1862

General Jackson moved from near Strasburg this morning toward Winchester. The report that the enemy had abandoned Winchester, with the exception of two or three regiments was generally believed by the soldiers and it was thought there would be no engagement in reoccupying the town.

About ten o'clock the enemy commenced advancing and drove out skirmishers back a mile or two towards Newtown.

The troops, having arrived soon after the middle of the day, the line of battle was immediately formed and the artillery of both armies commenced their fire which was kept up for several hours. This did but little damage to our side, wounding one or two and killing two or three horses.

The regiments in crossing an open field to their positions were in full and easy range of the enemy's guns. A number of shots were fired at the 4th but all fell a short distance off, without doing any damage.

Late in the afternoon our skirmishers were driven in and the fight became general along the whole line. Our men were eager for the fight and advanced most impetuously, and poured their fire into the ranks of the enemy, driving them completely from the field.

Heavy reinforcements were immediately brought forward by the Federals so completely out-numbering us that we were compelled to retire and leave them masters of the field.

Our forces were sheltered by a skirting of woods and the nearby brow of a hill and a little in advance of the latter, a stone fence.

In this place the Fourth fought and the enemy say was most hardly contested, and the dead lay in heaps. Our troops fought most gloriously and their deeds are the theme of admiration on enemy tongue. The Liberty Hall volunteers fully sustained the reputaton they gained at Manassas: no one quailed under the storm of bullets around him but fired upon them to the last. They with the Pulaski Guards were the last to leave the stone fence, the regiment having gone without our knowing it--and only left when the enemy gained the top of the hill to our right, and pouring a deadly fire down the wall and parallel with it.

Captain Morrison, finding the two companies left to themselves, ordered them to retire. We found the whole army giving way rapidly and the enemy firing upon them as they went and everyone endeavoring to make good his own safety.

After having retreated half a mile beyond the branch, I saw the cavalry of the enemy rapidly dashing upon us; I immediately turned off to the right (left wing of the enemy) hoping they would not pursue a single man but on they came and 4 or 5 calling on me to stop; not doing that they fired several shots at me, none of which however took effect, but by this time they were so close on me that I found further efforts useless and surrendered my gun and cartridge box and was marched back by two of them while the others still pursued the flying rebels.

I had gone a short distance only when I fell in with Captain Morrison and Lieutenant Lyle, whom I had just left as the cavalry charged upon us.

It was the First Virginia Cavalry who took us and escorted us with over a hundred others to Winchester where we were confined in the basement of the Episcopal Church. Others were lodged in the Court House, jail, etc. The whole number of prisoners is about 230, besides the wounded who could not be removed from the field.

We reached Winchester about 9 o'clock. The citizens sent us plenty of provisions for supper, having been furnished nothing by our captors. The room being crowded, there was but little opportunity to sleep or rest in any way.

Breakfast was also provided by the citizens--bread, meat, butter, coffee, milk, etc, were supplied to all.

Blankets and clothing were also being brought in, but soon prohibited.

Shortly after the middle of the day, we were ordered to "fall in" and were marched off--we knew not where, but were soon relieved of any doubt as we went direct to the depot, where we were put on the cars (freight) as fast as the roll could be called, and started for "Yankee Land." It was by no means a pleasing thought.

To go North, to be imprisoned in some prison or fort, to receive the contempt and supercilious treatment of Lincoln's tyrannical and despotic officers was humiliating and goading beyond expression. To have a yankee soldier and most probably a foreigner at that, with musket or drawn saber in hand, march one about at every call, was so offensive as to be borne with no sort of ease or compromise.

When performing the same offices for Federal prisoners in our hands, I had often wondered how I could bear it if I should ever fall into their hands. And now it was to be realized. I was to be put to the test.

I was to have them speak to me as peremptorily as to a negro, never once saying "will you do this" or "do that if you please," but always "stand over here," get back there," "pull off your hats," etc.

The cars were so crowded that the men could hardly seat themselves.

Reaching Harpers Ferry after dark, we remained there until morning, Tuesday 25th.

March 25, 1862

Started again this morning for Baltimore. On account of there being no windows in the cars, the view of the beautiful scenery was almost entirely lost.

At Jefferson's rock, I had a very important sight from the door of the car, by looking over the top of it. A temporary bridge has been thrown over the Potomac here, completing the connection of the Baltimore and Ohio railroad.

For several miles down the river the country is hilly and rough, but after leaving the river, it becomes level and gently hilly and very beautiful and rich.

Arriving in Baltimore about the middle of the afternoon, we halted at the Camden Station; being unexpected visitors, but few people were about; but soon it became known that a lot of Rebel prisoners had come to town; and several thousands quickly assembled, eager to catch a glimpse of such deluded creatures and rebellious subjects. However not a few among the throng came to look upon their friends and those who were battling for liberty and freedom.

All communication was prohibited, as far as possible, but questions would be asked and answered. "Throw me a button" said one; "a postage stamp," another; a third, a "Confederate note," etc.

At length we were formed in line and marched down Howard to Madison street and thence to the City jail for lodgings. All the way the crowd grew more dense and more boisterous, until we reached the jail, where the ladies waved their handkerchiefs and the children hurrahed for Jeff Davis.

We were confined in the North end of the building. It is very strong and most conveniently arranged.

Having had nothing to eat since breakfast at Winchester, our appetites were proportionately craving. The long wished for supper came at last--and such a supper, bread and coffee--at least what they called coffee, a concoction of rye sweetened with with molasses, a nauseating mixture, tasting as if there were something rotten in it. Next thing wanted was sleep, which was to be had by the two in each room, sleeping on a single cot. Worn out by loss of sleep + travel, I had a tolerably comfortable night's rest.

March 26th--Wednesday

Having passed my first night in Baltimore and in jail, I rose much refreshed, made my toilet, and waited breakfast, in hopes of getting something palatable, but it was only a repetition of the supper, the same hard bread and detestable coffee.

There was no recourse left but to wander about the building or sit down in my cell and think over my condition, of how long I would be there, whether we would be exchanged or paroled, and other kindred thoughts.

The papers were filled with glaring falsehoods and gross exaggerations of the late battle. The mind of the northern people would be so perverted by its first details that it would never know the real results of the engagement.

Kind friends in the city sent us numerous articles of clothing during the day, shirts, drawers, shoes, blankets, etc. For dinner we had bread and soup with a chunk of beef in it. Supper, nothing. Neither knives, forks, plates or spoons were to be had.

The "Clipper," noticing the arrival of prisoners, said they were lodged in the jail and "rations issued to them to which they were strangers." We were indeed strangers to such rations as we received.

March 27th

This day was passed in about the same way as the day before. All were eager to get the papers for further details of the fight and to know the position of General Jackson.

The papers, throughout the North, hailed the event as a glorious victory for the federal armies and illustrating the prowess of the Yankee mercenaries, but never mentioning the 23,000 men in their army which a Federal Captain boasted they had as soon as we were captured, and which all were very anxious to bring down to six or eight thousand when they found out the smallness of our force.

Correspondents of the press doubled and even trebled our force, boldly asserting that Shields had defeated Jackson's, Smith's and Longstreet's united forces. This unmitigated lie has been circulated far and wide, throughout the North, and will doubtless be believed by the mass of the Northern people.

Marvelous stories were told of Bank's pursuit and, and Jackson's disastrous retreat. Banks was shelling them all the way, with immense slaughter, and capturing 700 army wagons (2 or 3 times the number Jackson had) and Jackson was throwing the wounded and the dead from the ambulances and wagons, literally strewing the whole way with them, to facilitate his retreat.

The special correspondent of the "Herald" said that Jackson's forces was 16 regiments of infantry, numbering 11,000 men and three battalions of horse, under Ashby and Stuart; when in truth there were but 9 regiments and 1 battalion, less than 5,000 men and one regiment of horse (Ashby's)

Every effort was made to increase our force and diminish theirs.

Friday, March 28th

Crowds of ladies and gentlemen daily assembled without the walls where a view could be had of the windows of the jail and testified their sympathy and support by every demonstration in their power. Those known to sympathize with secessionists were prohibited admittance to the jail; but still they found many ways of ministering to our comfort and necessities, for which I (and I doubt not, all the prisoners) will ever feel most grateful. It was a cheering thought that there were many thousands of our friends in the city, ready and anxious to assist with their means and cheer us with their sympathy in our lonely confinement. It was rumored that we would be transferred to Fort Delaware.

March 29, 1862

Received notice this morning that we would go to Fort Delaware at 10 o'clock but the hours passed without any sign of movement, of which we were not sorry, for we preferred to remain in the city of our friends, rather than go to a fort, shut off from all the world by water as well as by its walls and be among our enemies.

Such sympathy was expressed and so much excitement stirred up through the city, that the authorities feared another outbreak of the populace (as on the 19th of April 1861) if they moved the prisoners through the streets in daylight. The keepers of the prison expressed this opinion to our officers.

The morning was cloudy and soon commenced snowing. In the afternoon we were again ordered to be ready to leave and started between 4 and 5 o'clock, under the escort of the 111th Pennsylvania Regiment.

Stormy and cold as it was, a large crowd was assembled at the gate to witness our departure. As soon as the prisoners appeared, the crowd commenced waving handkerchiefs, shouting and hurraing for Jeff Davis and the South. As we moved off, the crowd became perfectly uproarious, shouting and hallooing at the top of their voices. Many ladies were among the throng, some standing in carriages, from which they waved us a silent adieu, sometimes with a fervent "God bless you."

We marched up Calvert street, thence along the latter to the boat landing in which we were to take passage to the Fort.

The police (appointees of General Dix) were in attendance, some; of whom were very nice men, but the most of them bear a cruel and malignant hate towards the Rebels and delight to taunt and vex them in every way.

Some ladies were standing in the hall one day, asked the prisoners for a button; one was thrown out and fell at their feet, a policeman standing by grabbed it up and refused to let her have it.

We were soon aboard, all crowded into the lower part of the boat where nothing could be seen, and steamed down th bay to Fort Delaware.
