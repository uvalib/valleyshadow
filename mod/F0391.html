Franklin County: Christian Geisel to Louisa Geisel, April 22, 1862

Summary:
Geisel writes to tell his family that his unit is on the move and he will be out of contact for awhile.

Louisa Geisel

April 27th

Warrenton Junction, Va.

Dear Sister Louisa

I received your welcome letter of March 31st and it gave me great pleasure to hear from you. As you will see by the heading we have left Bell Blain. I intended to answer your letter before we left there but could not get time to do so. We left our camp near Bell Blain Landing on the 13th[illeg.] and marched up along the Rappahannock river with all the rest of the cavalry in the army of the Potomac, and some horse artillery; on the evening of the 14th we arrived at Beathon Station on the Orange and Alexandria R. R. near which place we were to cross the river, but it commenced to rain that night and the river swelled so that it was not fordable next day. the rebels in the mean time got aware of our intentions, and again the

[page 2]
roads were fit march again they had made preparations to prevent our crossing at that point. On monday the 20th we continued our march again up the river but it commenced to rain again and the roads got so bad that our reserve artillery could not get along and we halted that night near Warrenton having no supply train with us and being delayed so long by the unfavorable weather we had to fall back to Warrenton Junction for more supplies, at which place we are at present. As we expect to march again tomorrow I thought I would write you a few lines to inform you of our whereabouts, not knowing when I may have another opportunity as we will have no more communication after we start from here until this campaign is over; you will perhaps not hear from me for some time, but I will write to you as soon as an opportunity will present itself, if I am spared to do so.

[page 3]
We have undoubtedly some pretty hot work before us; where we are going is not publicly known, but the general opinion is here that we are to get rear of the rebel army of the Rappahannock and cut off their communications from Richmond, while Gen. Hooker will attack them with the Infantry. We have had splendid weather here since saturday.

I am very much pleased with the letter Frankilin sent me. it is written and composed better than many letters I have seen written by grown up persons, I am sorry that I cannot send him an answer today but he must excuse me as circumstances will not permit me to do so. Give my love to him and all the rest of the family. I must now come to a close hoping that you may all be in as good health as I enjoy at present.

I remain Your affectionate Brother

Christian Geisel

Please excuse haste
