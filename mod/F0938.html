Franklin County: William S. Garvin to Simon Cameron, December 4, 1862

Summary:
William S. Garvin recounts the bravery of his son, mourns the loss of his youngest daughter, and advises Cameron on Democratic support of a candidacy for senator.

Simon Cameron

Dec. 4, 1862

Mercer

Dear Gen.

In answer to your request for my son's name, (which is Edwin L.) I take the liberty of sending you his letter to me describing his share in the battle of Fair Oaks. I intended also to send you the copy of a letter of his cousin, Capt. Oliver C. Garvin, to his father, describing a part which Ed's modesty would not permit him to write to me - the fact, that when the Irish brigade would not advance on their famous charge, but squatted behind the rail road, he took their colors and put them up in the advance, in the midst of a shower of bullets from the enemy. Also, when Gen. Sumner inquired of his Col. (Frank) for an officer of intelligence and courage to perform a service outside of the [unclear: Pines], Edwin was called upon - that the General, when he was presented turned to the Col. and said it was too dangerous a service to send him on,

[page 2]
and then turned to Ed, and said, "I will remember you, sir, in the future." The letter containing all this has been mislaid, and hence I have to recount its contents. I say this, and send his letter, to prove, if you will take the trouble to read it, that he is not unworthy the favor you did me and him, in giving him an appointment in the regular army. The letter I send, I wish returned to me, for it is highly valued at home. The addrefs of my son is

Edwin S. Garvin
1st U.S. Artillery
New Orleans

I am sorry to hear that Mrs. Cameron did not enjoy good health while in Europe. I had supposed that Russia was not so unpleasant in the summer, and that it might have agreed with all of you. During your absence I received an awful blow in the death of my youngest daughter, and the only one

[page 3]
in my house. Home is no longer to me what it was. She died in June, and yet daily and sometimes hourly, is her image before me.

If I can, I will visit you this winter, if you will provide a pass for me.

I read to [unclear: Rose] that part of your letter which referred to him, and I can assure you that he was very much gratified. He now thinks that the appointment will be given to him on that the force arrayed in his favor will be irresistible.

In my last, I told you the democrats believed you could be Senator, if you choose. That is still their belief. But the man who leaves his party to support you, will need be a very bold man - for they all know that the democracy just now would be unrelenting in its wrath, and hence I make the remark. Give my respect to Mrs. Cameron

Yours

Wm. S. Garvin

To Senator Cameron
