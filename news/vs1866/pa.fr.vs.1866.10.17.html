The Result
(Column 1)
Summary: After acknowledging that the Democrats were soundly defeated in the election, the editor provides a litany of excuses to account for the thrashing, including the "desperate and unscrupulous" tactics used by the Republican leadership, the corrupt use of money, and the fraudulent votes of foreigners.
Full Text of Article:

It would be useless to disguise that we feel considerably disappointed at that result of the recent election. We confidently expected it to be otherwise. But the unscrupulousness of our opponents and the corrupt means to which they reacted again proved successful and disappointed our sanguine hopes.

In this county Geary's majority is 193, carrying with it the whole Republican county ticket. Sharpe is defeated in the district by 633. Col. Stumbaugh runs behidd his ticket in this county something over 100 votes, leading Col. McAllen, his competitor, 86 in the county. He and Shaman are both re-elected however, Perry giving about 100 against ur. The returns from the State are not all in yet, but the indications are that the majority for Geary will be something less than 15,000.

It is never a profitable business after an election is over, to speculate as to the causes which probably led to the result, but they are so apparent in this case that we may be pardoned for glancing at them. We had never known the Republican leaders to be so desperate and unscrupulous as they were prior to this election. No falsehood was too great to utter if they thought a vote could be gained for their party. It is admitted by all that it was the large vote that defeated us in this county. The increased Republican vote was secured mainly by the circulation among a certain class of people of the most unmitigated falsehoods. These people, many of whom are owners of United States securities, were told that if the Democratic party succeeded the rebel debt would be assumed and these securities would depreciate and perhaps become entirely worthless. That any portion of our citizens should believe such stuff is almost incredible, and speaks very poorly for their intelligence, but it is nevertheless true that very many who have not heretofore been in the habit of voting, were driven to the polls by such means. If the mad spirit of radicalism should succeed, as we fear it will, in precipitating another war upon the country, we hope these pocket patriots will have a happy time with their "bonds."

In addition to this wholesale lying of the Republican leaders, the corrupt use of money and [UNCLEAR] foreign voters in certain districts aided largely in our defeat. In this town votes were openly and shamelessly purchased at prices ranging from five to twenty dollars. In other districts the same means were used to corrupt the people in order to ensure a party triumph. Where money failed, threats and intimidation were resorted to, such as rich land owners threatening to turn of their poor tenants if they had failed to vote the Republican ticket. Minors were suborned to commit perjury in order to get in their votes, strangers were [UNCLEAR] in some districts and almost every species of villany and [UNCLEAR] was practiced to secure a Republican victory. Men voted the Republican ticket in this borough whom nobody knew except those who brought them here to vote, and who left early the next morning, perhaps never to return. In Green township, we are informed by citizens of that township who have had every opportunity to know every voter in that township, that men were brought to the polls to vote whose faces had never been seen there before, and who had no known residence in the district. And this , from the accounts we have, was the case elsewhere throughout the county. And what is true of Franklin county is doubtless true of the States generally. By such [UNCLEAR] and fraudulent [UNCLEAR] did our opponents, and the opponents of peace and Union, succeed in gaining a temporary triumphs in this State.

But these things cannot always continue. The party, sooner, or later, will break down of its own corruption. The principle of the Democratic party must ultimately prevail, or else free government will be a failure. The one cannot long exist without the other. All the party has to do is to keep up its organization and prepare for the next contest. We feel assured that time in the end will vindicate the justice of its cause and give [UNCLEAR] to the grand old historic party of the country.

The Democratic party, notwithstanding its defeat, has no [UNCLEAR] for despondency. It cast more votes on Tuesday a week for Hiester Clymer than were ever before cast in this state for a candidate for Governor. This shows the extraordinary vitality of the party. Nothing but the immense vote polled and the desperate means resorted to by its opponents could have defeated it. Under ordinary circumstances it would have been triumphantly successful. A party that can poll over 4,100 votes in the county, and 300,000 in the State, is far from being dead. It has exhibited a power and vitality which even its enemies will respect.

If the result of this election were nothing more than the defeat of the Democratic party, we should have but few regrets, but when we consider its probable effect on the future of our beloved country, we confess that our mind is filled with forebodings of evil. We hesitate to tear aside the curtain which hides the future from our gaze. It makes us shudder to think of the horrors, disasters and ruin which radicalism may have yet in store for us as a people.

The Lancaster Intelligencer truly says: "The people were fully and faithfully warned against the infamous schemes of the bold bad men who are now in the [UNCLEAR] throughout the North. They have allowed themselves to be duped and deluded into the support of the [UNCLEAR] which must affect disastrously all the best interests of this nation. That they will reap the reward of their folly we verily believe, but with that conviction is mingled the sad assurance that the nation must suffer for the foolishness of those who refused to heed the warnings of history, and the earnest entreaties of the wisest and purest statesmen of the land.

The future of this country is shrouded into gloom, and God alone knows how we shall escape the perils which now beset us.--There is no reason to believe that the Radicals will exhibit more moderation or decency in the future than they have displayed in the past. The Conservative men of the nation are the nation's hope, and they stand ready to do all that men can do to avert the evil which threatens. It is their duty to stand by the right, and they will do so as firmly in the future as they have in all the past."


Southern Manufacturing Interests
(Column 2)
Summary: Relying on the words of Governor Patton, of Georgia, to substantiate his argument, the writer of the article discusses the necessity of resurrecting southern industry and the impact of the region's growth on the future prosperity of the nation as a whole.
Origin of Article: Age
Full Text of Article:

The necessity of adopting a national policy that will at once re-establish the old order of things in the Southern States, is strikingly apparent when the industrial intrests and capacities of that section of the nation are carefully considered. Independent of the cotton, rice, sugar, tobacco, pitch, tar and turpentine that are furnished from the late revolted States, they have admirable facilities for manufactories. Before the war the attention of certain capitalists in Georgia and Alabama had been turned to the question of manufacturing in those States and some cotton mills had been erected and put in operation there. Of course, the war put a stop to all enterprise of that kind, but now the matter is again agitated; and Governor Patton of Alabama, has written a letter to the manufacturing companies in that State and Georgia which is full of important facts and suggestions. Although the local application of the Governor's letter is intended for his own State still general conclusions are for the most part as applicable to the people of Mississippi and to large portions of Louisiana and Texas.

After a full and minute survey of the general situation and future prospects of Alabama with respect to labor, Governor Patton declares that it will not be wise or expedient for the people to depend upon immigration to supply the deficiency occasioned by the diminution of black agricultural labor. He thinks it will be necessary for an indefinite period to rely mainly upon the growth and cultivation of cotton in negro hands, no matter how diminished the numbers may be by recent occurrences. The Governor urges the use of all the laborers in the State. Those not available for that cultivation of cotton, he says, may be turned to good account in the local manufacturing of cotton goods after the raw material has been produced. The additional value which may thus be imparted to cotton seems almost fabulous. But the results attainable are matters of statistical certainty. The value of cotton can thus be doubled, at the lowest calculation. The process is thus stated by Governor Patton:

It may be stated as a general proposition and at the same time a simple one that upon an average, one yard of cotton goods is worth as much as a pound of raw material. The number of yards produced from a single pound, depends, of course, upon the quality of the manufactured article. For instance, from one pound of cotton, you make four yards of what is known, in mercantile parlance, as four quarter brown muslin. These four yards would be worth eighty cents; and thus we see that the process of manufacturing has added sixty cents to the value of one pound of cotton. Taking this quality of goods as a taste of calculation, we can plainly see how the value of a given amount of raw material may be increased in value, until the finer article, a single pound may be made to yield several dollars. But we [UNCLEAR] and simple inducements for manufacturing enterprises by referring to the plainest and simplest articles that can be made. We double the value of cotton by converting into yarns, or common osnaburgs. Hence, we may take our two hundred and fifty thousand bales worth twenty-five millions of dollars, and if we manufacture nothing but the most ordinary articles, we may increase its value to fifty millions; that is as much as [UNCLEAR] realized from our former crop of one million bales.

The Governor argues that is the policy of the people living in the cotton growing region of the South to swell by every [UNCLEAR] in their power the ultimate value of that production. To do this in the most through and remunerative manner, they must combine manufacturing with planting, and thus reap a profit from both. What the extent of these profits may be under a proper system, can be seen in the following statement made by Governor Patton:

There are at Lowell ten large manufacturing establishments, all of which are chartered corporations. The aggregate capital invested in these ten mills is thirteen millions of dollars. The amount of cotton consumed is one hundred thousand bales; the number of yards produced, (exclusive of yarns,) something over a hundred millions and the number of operatives twelve thousand. The operative are mostly women and girls. It would require at least thirty thousand field laborers to raise this amount of cotton, and yet it is converted into yarn and cloths by twelve thousand operatives. The process of manufacturing the cotton as we have seen, will double its value, even if nothing but the plainest and commonest articles be made; and yet this process requires thirty thousand hands in the field to furnish the raw material.

These are plain and positive figures, taken from sources of undoubted reliability. It will be seen at once that the dividends paid upon these Lowell mills must be highly remunerative. I have before me a comparative list of the dividends recently declared for several of the incorporated factories at Lowell, and elsewhere in the North, and they range from twenty to forty per cent. per annum. It would, perhaps, be save to place the average at thirty per cent., thus showing that the annual profits equal nearly one-third of the capital invested. No dividend which I have seen reported is as low as the legal interest of Alabama. Statistics such as these tell their own story.--They are safe and unerring guides to all who may seek to ascertain the profits of manufacturing and sure they afford ample encouragement for Southern enterprise of this character.

The natural advantage for manufacturing which exist in the South are then presented in a plain and forcible manner. One of these is a difference of climate, which relieves the Southern manufacturer of large expense for the creation of an artificial temperature necessary for the successful running of machinery and the comfort of the operatives. In regard to water and fuel, the South is favored by nature in a remarkable degree. But the most prominent of all the advantages possessed by the South in the business of manufacturing, Governor Patton finds in the fact, that she produces all the raw material needed for that purpose on the spot. The greatness of this advantage may be judged from the difference of not less than about ten cents a pound in the price of cotton at Montgomery, Selma, or some other interior town, and the prices at New York or Liverpool. According to Governor Patton's reasoning, indeed, it would appear as unreasonable hereafter for the South to send her cotton for manufacture to New England, as for England to export all her iron, her flax, or her wool for the same purpose, ignoring Sheffield, Birmingham, Manchester, or other seats of local manufacture, or for France in like manner to ignore Lyons and other of her great manufacturing towns by exporting all her raw silk.

Those who look at our country as a unit and recognize the fact that by stimulating, in a just and proper manner, the productions and labor of one section, the whole is benefited, will be interested in this letter of Governor Patton, and his manly effort to build up an important branch of national development and wealth in the Southern States. It may be premature to think of the South as sending the inner articles of cotton manufactures to England, or China, or Japan, at this time, or within the next five years. But within that time, if they act upon the suggestions of Governor Patton, they can supply Mexico, the West Indies, the South American States, and even Philadelphia, New York, Boston, and Baltimore with cotton goods, such as are needed and will command a ready sale at paying prices for the manufacturer. This is the first point to be reached, and Governor Patton has shown that it is clearly within the range of possibility. This distinguished gentleman deserves credit for his efforts to bluid up the material resources of the South, and if the Radical politicians will cease their wicked attempts to destroy the nation, Northern capital will soon flow in that direction to aid in building manufactories and putting them in operation.--Age
