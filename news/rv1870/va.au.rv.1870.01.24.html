[No Title]
(Column 01)
Summary: The paper discusses provisions of the bill readmitting Virginia to the Union that the editors consider punitive.
Full Text of Article:

The Bill for the admission of Virginia, passed by both Houses of Congress, will be found elsewhere. When Virginia occupied an acknowledged place in the Union, it was a Union of Sovereign States, with equal rights. To day she is re-admitted to a place among the States of the Union, not the old Union which she assisted to establish but a new concern. Her act of admission is also a penal statute.

It contains conditions to which no other State in the Union is subjected; that she will never do certain things which other States have the full power to do. It prescribes an oath to be taken by her officers and Legislators, which obtains in the case of no other State, and attaches a penalty of imprisonment and fine for taking said oath improperly, and yet it is claimed that the Union to which she is now admitted is a Union of "equal States, with equal rights and privileges."

Well it may be, but we confess our inability to see it, and are more than pleased that there has been no uproarious rejoicing over the re-admission of Virginia on the terms and conditions required.

The only part that we are disposed to rejoice over, is the fact that Virginia has not been required to give her assent to the terms of the Bill of Admission, for God knows she has eaten dirt enough already to sicken a dozen "Mothers of States and Statesmen."

There may be some who are disposed to exult at the mere restoration of Virginia, to whom we commend the following summary of what she has been required to do or bear, in order to secure the boon (?) of restoration:

1. The ratification of the 13th amendment to the Federal Constitution, abolishing slavery.

2. The repeal of her ordinance of secession.

3. The repudiation of the Confederate debt.

4. The adoption of the Bayne-Underwood constitution, giving the ballot to all male negroes twenty-one years old, and making them all eligible to office; and giving them control in a large proportion of the counties of the State.

5. The election of a Republican delegation to Congress.

6. The election of a Republican State ticket.

7. The ratification of the 14th amendment to the Federal Constitution.

8. The ratification of the 15th amendment to the Federal Constitution.

9. An oath from every member of the State Legislature, and every executive and judicial officer of the State, to the effect that he does not come within the terms of the 3d clause of the 14th amendment.

10. That the constitution of the State shall never be amended so as to prevent persons from voting who are paupers, or who may not have paid their taxes, or who may not know how to read or write.

11. That the said constitution of the State shall never be altered so as to deprive any one of the right to hold office on account of color.

12. That the said constitution shall never be changed so as to deprive the colored people of equal rights and privileges in connection with the public schools of the State.

After rolling this delightful morsel under their tongues, if they be still disposed to rejoice, why let them do it. Halloo at the top of your voices, gentlemen, and let the world hear how loud you can shout, and for how little.

Sad Accident
(Column 01)
Summary: An "old gentleman" named George White fell from the railroad bridge while attempting to cross it. Mr. Erman, an attendant at the Western Lunatic Asylum, found him and carried him to the jail where several surgeons attempted to save his life to no avail. A man named Mahany was arrested for throwing white from the bridge and was released on $300 bail.
(Names in announcement: Erman, Mahany, George White)
[No Title]
(Column 01)
Summary: On Friday night, $50 worth of leather was stolen from the tan-house of Mr. T. D. Herron and a horse was stolen from the stable of William A. Hanger. The South River Horse Thief Detective Club recovered the horse that was found loose in the woods of John A. Harman. A man arrested an African American man who attempted to sell leather resembling the stolen items. He was taken before the Mayor but released for lack of evidence. Herron later identified the leather as his.
(Names in announcement: T. D. Herron, William A. Hanger, John A. Harman)
